ELEPHANTS
& RHINOS

Earth’s Ecosystem Engineers

Elephants are often considered keystone species, meaning they have a disproportionately large impact on their environment relative to their abundance. Keystone species play a critical role in maintaining the structure of an ecological community. Without them, the ecosystem would be dramatically different or cease to exist altogether.

+
Habitat Configuration

These megaherbivores significantly alter landscapes by moving through them. Pulling down trees, breaking up bushes, and stomping through vegetation sets off a chain reaction of events that benefit the greater wildlife community. Whether creating grassland spaces for zebras to graze or opening gaps in forest canopies to provide light for low-lying plants to grow, elephants and rhinos provide countless species with spaces to co-exist and thrive.

Healthy Water Systems

Their tree clearing patterns creates new water sources, further transforming the environment. Rivers meander into new pathways, carve out fresh landscapes, and redistribute water and nutrients across the ecosystem–essential to regions that rely on elephant and rhino-modified ecosystems for water resources. Watering holes help other species survive droughts.

Positive Vegetation Impact

Modified water flow nourishes surrounding vegetation and fosters the growth of plants in areas previously shaded by dense canopy cover. Lush habitats rich in biodiversity emerge, supporting an array of plant species and providing sustenance for wildlife.

Biodiversity Increases

Abundant vegetation draws herbivores such as impala and zebras to these verdant oases, shaping diverse plant communities through browsing behaviors.

Strong Ecosystem Balance

With elephants and rhinos at the helm of habitat configuration, they initiate a domino effect that promotes balance in predator-prey dynamics. Thriving plant communities bring the herbivores, which then attract a variety of predators, weaving together an intricate web of life within the ecosystem.

Carbon Sequestration & Seed Dispursal

As the gardeners of the forest, they play a vital role in carbon sequestration through their foraging and movement habits. By thinning forests of low carbon density trees and plants, they help sunlight reach larger trees that capture more carbon from the atmosphere. They contribute to the food chain by dispersing seeds through waste (a nutrient-rich fertilizer) across vast distances. Seed dispersal helps distribute plants across the landscape for other herbivores to eat, facilitates the regeneration of forests, and creates microhabitats.

+
Habitat Degradation

Habitat complexity would be minimized without the creation of micro-habitats that support a wide range of plant and animal species. Trails may become overgrown, restricting easy access to water and food. Animals that rely on the browsing and foraging activities of elephants and rhinos to live, eat, and reproduce would be in jeopardy. If we lose elephants and rhinos, we are at risk of losing those who depend on them.

Altered Water Systems

Without elephants and rhinos to shape landscapes, water systems would be disrupted, potentially leading to stagnation, erosion, and altered hydrological patterns. Animals that rely on watering holes–and giant water-filled footprints–would lose their habitats and sources of water for survival. Changes in the availability of water for both wildlife and human communities could exacerbate water scarcity issues.

Negative Vegetation Impact

Habitats may become barren and ecologically impoverished without the presence of elephants and rhinos to modify water flow. Vegetation would decline, leading to reduced growth of plants and loss of biodiversity.

Biodiversity Decreases

The absence of abundant vegetation would lead to a decline in herbivore populations and have cascading effects on predator populations.

Imbalanced Ecosystem

Elephants and rhinos are dominant forces in shaping the composition and structure of entire ecosystems. Their extinction would disrupt the interconnectedness of life within habitats, leading to potential collapses in ecosystem function and resilience.

Negative Impact on Climate Change

Elephants and rhinos play a seismic role in protecting our planet. Whether contributing to natural carbon capture or paving the way for new growth, they have a giant impact on the overall health of our world. Without these landscape architects, many tree species will be left without a means to spread their seeds. Ecosystems may become less resilient to environmental stressors, potentially exacerbating the effects of climate change on biodiversity and ecosystem function.

MINI ECOSYSTEMS

Towering Trees, Tiny Refugees

Trees damaged by browsing elephants take on a new purpose: shelter. Hollows and twisted crevices within elephant-damaged trees become homes for lizards and small mammals to inhabit. They provide a respite from predators and harsh environments, and serve as breeding grounds for new life.

An Entire Ecosystem in a Single Footstep

Small “ponds” created by massive footprints transform into micro-ecosystems and connectivity hubs for frogs and other vertebrate populations. These tracks enhance biodiversity and are a safe, stable space for tadpoles to grow from eggs to adults.

Guardians of Ocean Health

Explore the vital role of whale species in sustaining marine ecosystems and regulating global climate through nutrient cycling, oxygen production, and deep-sea biodiversity. The risks of their extinction impacts not only marine life but life on earth for humanity.

+
Harmful Algal Blooms (HAB)

A harmful algal bloom (HAB), or excessive algae growth, is an algal bloom that causes negative impacts to other organisms by pro-duction of natural toxins, mechanical damage to other organisms, or by other means. HABs can cause significant harm to animals, the environment and economies. They have been increasing in size and frequency worldwide, a fact that many experts attribute to global climate change.

Healthy Ecosystem Nutrient Cycling

Whales play a crucial role in the intricate nutrient cycling of oceanic ecosystems. Their vertical movement from ocean depths to the surface transports rich resources from the abyss, fueling the growth of marine life.

Positive Global Impact

Whales embark on extensive migrations across ocean basins, dispersing nutrients over vast distances. This global movement ensures the health and productivity of marine ecosystems, contributing to the remarkable biodiversity of our oceans.

Global Oxygen Production

Whales' role in nutrient cycling is closely tied to the production of oxygen. Marine photosynthetic organisms, reliant on nutrients transported by whales, generate approximately two-thirds of Earth's oxygen, highlighting the critical importance of whale conservation.

Climate Change Slowed

Climate change poses a significant threat to the stability of nutrient cycling in marine ecosystems. As surface waters warm, the natural stratification of the ocean becomes disrupted, impeding the vertical mixing of nutrients essential for sustaining life. This disruption can lead to intensified thermal stratification, further exacerbating the challenges faced by marine ecosystems. Whales help combat this through their nutrient cyling and global movement.

Whale Falls Help Ecosystems Thrive

When whales die, their bodies sink to the ocean floor, creating rich ecosystems known as "whale falls." These carcasses provide a bounty of nutrients to deep-sea organisms, fueling vibrant communities of scavengers and decomposers in the otherwise nutrient-poor abyssal zone.

+
Nutrient Cycling Disrupted

Without whales to transport nutrients from ocean depths to sur-face waters, the intricate nutrient cycling of marine ecosystems would be disrupted. This could lead to a decline in phytoplankton productivity and subsequently impact the entire marine food web.

Negative Global Impact

The extinction of whales would halt their extensive migrations across ocean basins, leading to a localized concentration of nutri-ents. This loss of global nutrient dispersion would diminish the health and resilience of marine ecosystems worldwide, threatening biodiversity.

Decreased Oxygen Production

With whales gone, the vital contribution they make to oxygen pro-duction through nutrient cycling would be lost. This could signifi-cantly reduce the amount of oxygen generated by marine photo-synthetic organisms, impacting not only marine life but terrestrial ecosystems as well.

Climate Change Hastened

The absence of whales could exacerbate global warming by dis-rupting the natural processes that regulate oceanic thermal stratifi-cation. Without efficient nutrient cycling facilitated by whales, sur-face waters may become warmer, further intensifying the challenges posed by climate change.

Ecosystems Fail Without Whale Falls

Whale falls, which sustain deep-sea ecosystems, would cease to occur in the event of whale extinction. This would deprive deep-sea organisms of essential nutrients, leading to a decline in biodiversity and potentially disrupting the balance of deep-sea ecosystems.

All life begins within our oceans

Nutrient Cycling Drives Ocean Life

Whales are extremely important to the overall nutrient cycling of the oceans, which in turn provides for the vast diversity of marine species. The means in which whales aid this essential cycle is known as the “whale pump” theory. By digesting zooplankton, and other food species and pumping out excrement, whales help keep key nutrients suspended close to the surface where they can power blooms of the carbon-absorbing phytoplankton species that form the base of ocean nutrient cycle.

Oceanic Phytoplankton Produce Oxygen

Phytoplankton species, like diatoms, use photosynthesis to convert carbon dioxide and seawater into oxygen and sugars for energy. This produces roughly 50% of all the oxygen on Earth. All of the plants and trees on land account for another 25% of that total. The last 25% is produced by marine kelp species known as large algae. This means that the oceans combine for about 75% of all the oxygen we breathe.

American
Buffalo

Climate Heroes & Microhabitat Creators

The American buffalo are considered keystone species, meaning they have a disproportionately large impact on their environment relative to their abundance. So what would happen if they disappeared from their ecosystems?

Seed / Nutrient Dispersal

Buffalo herds once numbered in the hundreds of thousands. Their total population was estimated to be in the 10s of millions. These numbers gave them great influence over the grasslands they roamed and grazed upon. Through their vast movement patterns, they aided in the spreading of seeds on both their fur and in their droppings. Their dung also harbored nutrients essential to keep the balance of the plant and animal species that they co-exist with in their ecosystem. The plants of the great plains have evolved with the Buffalo, and are best off when it is present with them in great numbers.

Climate Change Threats

Without herds of bison to roam and properly maintain the grassland ecosystems, non-native plant species take over for native keystone plant species and the effect is rapid imbalance. Native grassland species like Buffalo Grass and Prairie Dropseed, have deep (8+ feet) and robust root systems that do not die in winters and are vital for capturing carbon. Cattle graze grasslands too frequently and kill the indigenous vegetation over time, exposing the land to short-rooted species like Kentucky Blue Grass that don’t capture carbon and dies in winters.

Rainwater Captured in Wallows is Essential

Buffalo / Bison are famous for their massive grazing herds, but they are essential to their ecosystem for other reasons too. Bison create depressions in the ground when they roll around to cool off, shed winter coat fur, rid themselves of parasites, or just to burn off excess energy. These wallows are often visited year after year by the same herds, and this creates a sort of permenant intermittent wetlands as these depressions collect rainwater. Migrating ducks and geese use them to rest on their journeys north or south.

Positive Landscape Transformation

Buffalo make a large impact on their environment—literally. Their giant hooves aerate the soil, aid in seed dispersal, and alter vegetation structures to help plants grow. In the winter, bison use their massive heads, horns, and shoulders to plow through deep snow, creating necessary pathways for animals such as pronghorn antelope and elk. This uncovers living vegetation, making it more accessible to animals. When bison graze, they naturally engineer grass to varying heights–becoming ideal locations for different types of birds to build concealed nests.

Rich Habitat Creation

As buffalo graze, they disperse native seeds across the plains. This natural form of nutrient “recycling” fertilizes the soil, enabling more plants and animals to thrive. A myriad of vibrant ecosystems are created, becoming microhabitats for plant communities, insects, birds, and small mammals.

New Water Networks

Known as “wallowing,” buffalo roll in the dirt to send social signals, deter biting insects, and shed their fur—creating large bowl-shaped depressions in the ground. When abandoned wallows fill with rainwater, they become breeding sites for species including amphibians and migrating birds. The more compressed the soil, the more water the wallows can retain–providing vital drinking water sources for wildlife.

Increased Carbon Capture

When buffalo forage, they nurture plants above ground as well as the critical roots below. These roots are the powerhouses that draw harmful C02 out of the air, keep it sequestered in the ground, and send oxygen back into the atmosphere to help all forms of life on Earth survive.

+
Negative Landscape Degradation

Buffalo migration and foraging habits effect the landscape, which in turn effects the health and stability of organisms throughout the ecosystem. Without these unique movement patterns, birds and prairie dogs could not build camouflaged nests in grasslands. Larger mammals, like pronghorn antelope and elk, would experience more challenges traversing through harsh winter climates, without large herds of bison to clear the way. Vegetation would be inaccessible to many species.

Decreased Biodiversity

Without the buffalo's influence on soil, plants and microorganisms that rely on an optimal nutrient flow would face a less hospitable, resilient habitat. Substantially lower plant biodiversity would negatively impact the balance of the entire prairie ecosystem.

Fewer Water Sources

Wallows are important for plant and animal life cycles, especially when rainfall is unpredictable. Without these pools, water would only be available in existing streams and ponds. Prairie vegetation that requires a moist habitat would lack a suitable enviroment to grow. Amphibeans and migratory birds that rely on these ""mini-wetlands"" to eat and breed would be in danger.

In Symbiotic Balance with the Prairie

The American Buffalo is perhaps the most signifigant indigenous mammal in the known history of North America. Perfectly adapted to the land, through all seasons, and at one time, roamed wild from Florida, to the Yukon in numbers estiumated to be tens of millions. Not prone to overgrazing like modern cattle, the Buffalo is integral to the health of the grasslands and keystone plant species. Virtually every species of plant and animal on the prairie, benefits and thrives in balance when the buffalo numbers are healthy.

Creating Water Sources for an Ecosystem

Small animals like reptiles and birds (sage grouse, ducks, geese) and mammals like prairie dogs and pronghorn antelope need drinking water, and in the grasslands, rivers can be few and far between. The seasonal wallows that Buffalo create, are vital drinking water sources when they fill with rainwater that would otherwise seep into the ground and be unavailable for these species to drink.

Wolves

Ecosystem Regulators

Wolves are considered keystone species, meaning they have a disproportionately large impact on their environment relative to their abundance. So what would happen if they disappeared from their ecosystems?

A Balanced Food Chain

Wolves are the true top-down drivers of an ecosystem food chain, not bears. Wolves keep herbivores at healthy populations by focusing on the old or the sick. Wolf kills provide sustenance to a wide variety of animals, including bears, eagles, coyotes, and ravens.

Soil Eroded Riparian Zones

Extensive browsing by herbivores scours vegetation, making riverbanks less stable. Plants that many species depend on to survive become limited, particularly aspen groves, and willows that would otherwise thrive along riverbanks. This prevents the root systems that sustain riverbank structure, and aids erosion.

Beaver Dams Create Biodiverse Wetlands

Beavers use mud, grass, sticks, and trees as construction materials to build dams that serve many important purposes. They provide drinking water for mammals and breeding grounds and habitats for wetland species, including birds and fish. By modifying the environment, these beaver-made ponds control soil erosion, capture carbon, and support a variety of plants and wildlife.

Ecosystem Balance

As apex predators, wolves play a critical role in keeping ecosystems healthy and balanced. By hunting large grazers, including deer, moose, and elk, wolves help manage those populations--benefitting many plant and animal species.

Regulation of Herbivores

When wolves hunt, they initiate many positive ripple effects in their ecosystems. Not only do they reduce their prey's numbers, but they also influence their behavior. To avoid predator ambushes, elk and deer alter their foraging patterns and spend less time eating in one place. This allows trees and shrubs to grow--creating new habitats for birds, insects, beavers, and other species. Carcasses left by wolves provide nutrients for bears and other scavengers like ravens, eagles, magpies, and coyotes.

Thriving Vegetation

Without overgrazing of herbivores, riparian zones (the grasses, trees and shrubs along the edges of rivers and streams) can recover and support an abundance of plants and wildlife. There are more berries available for grizzly bears to eat as part of their diverse diet.

River Flow Transformation

The regenerating, dense riparian vegetation stabilizes the riverbanks, reduces flood water velocity, and helps rivers flow in new directions. Flourishing plants also provide habitats for birds, insects, and beyond–helping to maintain biodiversity.

Ecosystem Imbalance

Without wolves as their predator, herbivore populations increase–and natural resources to support their numbers decrease.

Overpopulation of Herbivores

Overpopulation of herbivores leads to overgrazing of grass, shrubs, and trees, which negatively impacts other wildlife species. Huge herds of elk, for example, can cause significant changes to lush riverbank landscapes, including trampled streams and habitat loss.

Habitat Degradation

When willow and aspen are overgrazed, small mammals can't hide from predators within dense plants. Bird populations decline when there are less trees to nest in. Beavers no longer have the tools to build their dams, which play a vital role in slowing the flow of streams and creating wetlands that serve as ecosystems for fish and other species.

Biodiversity Decreases

Without vegetation and beaver dams, there are fewer microhabitats for species to thrive, leading to a loss of biodiversity.

Keystone wardens of biodiversity balance

Wolves & Ravens Team Up to Hunt

Wolves and ravens have a well-documented symbiotic relationship. Ravens help wolves to find pray, and after wolf kills, have their pick of the remains before many other scavenger species get their chance. This behavior is referred to as mutualistic behavior. Both species bring benefits to the other. Ravens have even been observed interacting in playful ways with wolf pups. They are known as “wolf birds” due to their close relationship with wolves.

Preventing Herbivores from Overgrazing

With wolves present in an ecosystem, the herbivores spend less time grazing near water sources and rivers, and more time in higher-elevated regions where they can see further to keep an eye on predators. With herbivores like elk constantly wary of wolves, the raparian zones along rivers grow to healthy heights and their root systems protect riverbanks. The trees then provide shade for fish and other freshwater species.

A History of
Conservation

The Lineage of
Species Conservation

Conservation began as a form of applied science, with leaders from forestry, agronomy, geology and hydrology backgrounds playing pivotal roles. However, the public’s feelings toward and understanding of conservation was inherently dynamic — eventually creating years of political and social controversy.

Therefore, the conservation movement of the early twentieth century differs greatly from the environmental movement that arose after 1950, and even the version we have today. While early forms of conservation focused on scientifically planning the use of natural resources, environmentalists aimed to maintain species populations and the wild natural areas they inhabited.

A Movement is Born

Some historians argue that conservation began when British landscape architect, John Evelyn, presented his book “Sylva, or A Discourse of Forest Trees” to the Royal Society in 1662. And although his works established an important foothold, conservation efforts didn’t gain traction until the industrial era, nearly 100 years later.

GHOST WOLF COMMUNICATION STRATEGY 

The "ghost" wolves of the Gulf Coast are a unique population of wild canids that inhabit the coastal regions of Louisiana and Texas. While they resemble coyotes (Canis latrans), ghost wolves have physical characteristics atypical of conventional coyotes, such as overly long legs, broader heads, and reddish fur with white coloration on their muzzles. Genetic analyses have revealed that they carry substantial ancestry from the American red wolf (Canis rufus) population under human care in the S.A.F.E. (Saving Animals From Extinction) program. 

The term “ghost” wolf came after genetic studies revealed these animals carried genetics which are not present in any of the other North American canids, including the contemporary red wolves under human care or in the wild.

This body of research suggests that ancestral, pre-extinction red wolf “ghost” genes, thought to be extinct in the wild since the 1980s, persisted within these admixed populations. 

Historic red wolf range

The American red wolf is the only large carnivore species solely endemic to the United States and historically roamed extensively across the southeastern states. Unfortunately, by the mid-20th century, their numbers had plummeted due to habitat loss, hunting, and predator control programs. They survived only in a small coastal area in southeast Texas and southwest Louisiana. With extinction imminent, the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service began trapping wild canids in the area between 1973 and 1980 to establish a captive breeding program with the goal of eventual reintroductions into the wild. The red wolf was officially declared extinct in the wild in 1980. Reintroduction efforts from the captive population began in 1987 at the Alligator River National Wildlife Refuge in North Carolina, but the wild population remains precariously low, with fewer than 20 individuals estimated to remain—all descended from 14 founders, of which 12 were reproductively successful. The red wolf is the world’s most threatened wolf species.

GHOST WOLF COMMUNICAION STRATEGY 

4 PHASES

+

PHASE 1: GENETIC SEQUENCING 

To fully understand what constituted the historic American red wolf, the coalition is generating the world’s first high-quality reference genome of the red wolf, as well as sequencing historic Gulf Coast red wolf museum specimens such as fur pelts or bone fragments to identify lost genetic diversity. This process will create a genetic blueprint of both the ancestral and the present-day red wolf, and allow our team to compare those genomes with the genomes of ghost wolves today. The coalition will also sequence existing ghost wolves to pinpoint red wolf genetic markers and identify individuals with the highest proportion of ancestral and present-day red wolf ancestry. These ghost wolves will be critical because they possess valuable genetics necessary for the successful restoration of the ancestral Gulf Coast red wolf. 

+

PHASE 2: CLONING 

Ghost wolves with the highest percentage of ancestral red wolf genetics will be cloned to establish a genetically diverse insurance population and distinct breeding groups will be established to prevent genetic bottlenecking. This phase will utilize cross-species surrogacy, with domestic dogs serving as surrogate mothers, eliminating the need to remove ghost wolves from the wild, accelerating the cloning process, and improving success rates. Once the cloned pups are born, a co-rearing strategy involving both the surrogate dog mothers and human caretakers will ensure proper care and socialization, fostering important canid behaviors. The insurance population will be housed at a captive management facility located in the ghost wolves’ native range. 

PHASE 3: RESTORING ANCESTRAL TRAITS

Once a genetically robust insurance population has been established, multiplex gene-editing technology can be deployed to incorporate ancestral red wolf traits into future ghost wolf cell lines. Custom gene-editing tools would be designed to modify multiple genetic loci simultaneously, enabling the engineering of both ancestral and contemporary red wolf genes, and the elimination of coyote genetic introgression, in ghost wolf embryos. Viable embryos will then be selected for transfer into surrogates for gestation, producing genetically robust red wolves with ancestral Gulf Coast genetics. 

PHASE 4: CAPTIVE HEAD START & REWILDING 

The coalition is establishing an 80-acre captive management facility located at the Moore-Odom FR Ranch Preserve in southwestern Louisiana to provide the insurance population of ghost wolves with a safe environment to live within their native range. This facility will house the cloned ghost wolves and their future descendants as work progresses to restore ancestral red wolf traits, with the eventual goal of rewilding the Gulf Coast red wolf to its native habitat.

The absence of the gray wolf created a unique opportunity for prey animals—specifically Yellowstone National Park’s elk population. Without a predator to maintain balance, their numbers soared in response. And, because they no longer had a wolf to chase them, the elk simply didn’t run. As a result, hoards of hoofed herbivores stood in place, grazing an area until its last blade of vegetation was trampled or eaten. Slowly, the elk picked their environment clean, tipping the scales

of biodiversity with every bite. To park officials and conservationists alike, it seemed as though the disappearance of the wolf would forever scar the face of one of America’s most scenic natural assets. Fortunately, in the latter part of the century, the winds of change blew the gray wolf to the forefront of conservation. Recognizing their crucial role in maintaining ecological balance, efforts to reintroduce them—specifically in Yellowstone—emerged.

A DEVIL IN NAME ONLY

With a vision to rewild Australia, conservationists have looked to the Tasmanian devil. This apex predator, while keeping possum and wallaby populations in check, would drive out invasive foxes and cats, enabling native small mammals to recover. In parallel, it would help enrich the soil, spread seeds, bury leaf litter, and minimize the buildup of flammable material – dampening the breadth of forest fires. Twenty-six healthy Tasmanian devils have been reintroduced to mainland Australia to date. Returning the Tasmanian devil to Australia is a quintessential example of the positive feedforward impacts of species reintroductions.

Conservation
Meets Technology

By working on the de-extinction of the thylacine, Colossal is developing and advancing exciting new technologies that aid in the conservation of animals like the Tasmanian devil. With Colossal-born innovations like the marsupial exo-pouch, assisted reproductive technologies and cryopreservation , there’s a promising future for these endangered marsupials. These innovations will not only benefit the conservation and restoration of Tasmanian devil populations, but many other endangered marsupial predators, as well. Among them are northern and eastern quolls, Kangaroo Island dunnarts, numbats and pygmy mountain possums.

But there’s still hope. Our efforts can assist in building insurance populations for a multitude of endangered species, as well as help guarantee their long-term survival.

By using artificial wombs, it will be possible to breed endangered animals quickly, efficiently and without the limitations associated with traditional breeding programs. Utilizing artificial processes can expedite the revival of endangered populations, inherently preventing their extinction.

Exogenous Development

Also known as in vitro gestation or artificial womb technology, exogenous development involves the transfer of a developing embryo to an artificial environment. Specifically, a cultivated environment that is able to provide the embryo with all conditions necessary to develop and grow outside of its mother's body.

Both surrogacy and artificial wombs are viable solutions for bringing back extinct species. However, they’re equally advantageous in the conservation of those which are currently endangered, as well.

What’s unique about synthetic reproductive hardware is that it’s able to independently support the life of a developing organism in an ex utero atmosphere until it’s ready for birth. Colossal’s gestational technology presents conservationists with a promising opportunity to help endangered species at scale.
